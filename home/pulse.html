PULSE

Exploring a future where we can use plants as sensors

Pulse is a tool that reads and interprets the bioelectrical signals of plants, visualizing them in engaging and educational ways. The goal is to foster a deeper understanding of the natural world and inspire innovative approaches to environmental monitoring.

Initial research Question:

“What could we learn if we actually listened?”

How might we explore new areas of plant - computer interaction to speculate on interspecies collaboration?

How can we use modern technology to listen to and understand the bioelectrical signals of plants, and what insights can we gain from these signals about the health and behaviour of plants and ecosystems?

Current Prototype - PULSE 2.0

A handheld device that allows you to visualise the bio-electrical signals in plants

The current prototype of the PULSE 2.0 device

The current prototype is a concept handheld device and a circuit designed to detect, visualize, and turn the bioelectrical signals of plants into sound. The system consists of an Arduino-based circuit that reads electrical signals from electrodes attached to the plant. These signals are processed and converted into visual and audio outputs, allowing users to interact with and explore the hidden world of plant communication.

    • Arduino Uno: Central controller for processing signals.

    • 555 Timer: Modulates output based on plant conductivity.

    • Electrodes: Attached to the plant to read electrical signals.

    • Potentiometer: Adjusts signal amplitude.

    • Buzzer: Generates sound based on processed signals.

    • Volume Control Buttons: Adjusts the sound volume.

    • 3.5mm Audio Jacks: Connects electrodes and provides audio output.

    • Adafruit TFT Panel: Displays signal waveforms.

A potentiometer is used to adjust the amplitude of the bio signal received, allowing you to “tune” into the frequency of the plant.

Why would we want to do this?

“Could we explore a future where we can use plants as sensors?”

Our initial inspiration stemmed from interspecies collaboration. We were fascinated by projects turning plant signals into music and research on organic computing through mycelium networks. This led us to explore how much we could learn by "listening" to plants.

Our Initial Team

Our team included three product designers (including myself), an architect, an ecologist, and an IoT and UX designer. Each member brought unique skills to the table:

  • Marius (IoT and UX Designer): Instrumental in building our initial prototype circuit.

  • Albert (Ecologist): Provided insights on testing plant reactions to environmental changes.

  • Everardo, Nicolo, Jorge, Everardo & Myself (Architect & Product Designers): Focused on refining the circuit and adapting Arduino code to detect subtle plant signals.

How did we get here?

Exploration

Electrodes

Arduino

Plants

  • In the exploration phase, we discovered the extreme sensitivity of plant signals. Initially, our Arduino ECG sensors, designed for human heart rate detection, failed to capture the subtle electrical signals of plants. This realization led us to redesign our circuit and adapt the code for better signal detection. We placed electrodes on the leaf and stem of the plant to get accurate readings.

Eureka Moment!

We adapted some Arduino MIDI code, this finally allowed us to visualise the bioelectrical signals of the plant as it reacted to touch, water, sunlight etc.

  • A significant breakthrough came when we found a project on GitHub that turned plant signals into music using an Arduino MIDI. We reverse-engineered this project, which guided us in refining our circuit and code. This enabled us to detect real-time changes in plant signals in response to touch, water, and sunlight. This opened up new possibilities for visualizing these signals.

Key Learnings

  • One of the most significant learning moments was realizing how much we didn't know about detecting and interpreting plant signals. Initially, we were unsure whether we were reading actual plant signals or just noise. Through iterative testing and verification, such as using non-conductive materials to touch the plants, we confirmed the validity of our readings. This process underscored the importance of rigorous scientific methods and inspired us to develop controlled environments for further testing.

Stage 1 Prototyping & Development

Prototype 1 - Reading signals

Using Arduino to read bio - electrical plant signals

  • We started by analysing and modifying circuits from previous projects. Using breadboards, we tested various configurations with Arduino ECG sensors, aiming to keep the project open-source for community contribution. Our initial prototype used a 555 Timer in astable mode, capacitors, resistors, and Arduino to read plant signals and output them on the serial plotter.

testing out our prototype circuit

Software development

Reading the basics

  • Programmed in Arduino, the software we developed at this stage was designed to be able to take in readings from multiple electrodes attached to the plant, and print them on the serial plotter in the Arduino IDE programme.

    This was key, as it would allow us to later use these value readings to create exciting and interesting visuals that people could interact with.

Prototyping something new

After the initial success of the first Pulse Prototype and the end of this phase of the project during the course of Masters, I wanted to continue onwards and try and keep developing this idea into a standalone device that could be used as an educational tool for kids and adults to explore a new aspect in the world of plants.

Hardware Iteration

Developing a fully integrated system. Building out a fully functioning prototype for a handheld device.

  • Building on the first prototype, I integrated a potentiometer for amplitude adjustment, volume control buttons, and a speaker to turn the plant signal values into melodic tones. The new prototype also includes an Adafruit TFT panel to display the waveform of the plant signals. The idea is to enhance the user interaction and make the system more intuitive and fun.

    The values picked up by the electrodes would be displayed on a small OLED screen as a waveform. The potentiometer that allows for amplitude adjustment of the wave signal displayed on screen is designed to allow you as the user to “Tune” into the right frequency of the plant.

    As the electrodes attached to the plant pick up signals, those values are turned into melodic tones and played out loud through a speaker attached directly to the circuit or through a 3.5mm audio jack, allowing you to plug in a pair of headphones or a separate speaker.

    The aim of the final circuit prototype is to develop it into a shield that can be plugged into an Arduino Uno or Nano. This would then be slotted into a 3d printed casing to turn it into a fully standalone device.

Software upgrade

Tuning the signal and creating sound

  • To add an extra element to the prototype, the software has been updated to display the values read by the electrodes as a waveform on a small screen, and turn the values into melodic tones using an Arduino library called Mozzi. I wanted to include this feature as it gives you the space to not only develop all kinds of new projects, but also an extra method of exploring this hidden world of plants.

Where is the project now?

Right now the project is at the stage where the circuit is complete and functioning, and I can start developing the casing.

  • For this I wanted to create a rugged look, inspired by walkie talkies, satellite phones and Handheld Satnav devices. This was to create a durable design that will last for a long time (especially in the hands of kids) but also create the semiotic connotations of the outdoors environment it’s supposed to be used in.

    This tool aims to foster curiosity and understanding, especially among children, by enabling them to explore and interact with the natural world in new ways. It could be used in educational settings, such as biology classes and field trips, providing a hands-on learning experience.

Early concept development

Ideation and concept development for the casing and user journey of the PULSE 2.0 handheld device. Using a mixture of traditional sketching and emerging visual tools such as midjourney to render the hand drawn sketches.

  • The initial casing design has been done through a combination of initial rough ideation sketching and using midjourney to quickly develop and refine ideas through iterative generation using early sketches as base prompts to play around with shape, colour, material and finish.

    Using new tools like Midjourney have been an amazing for speeding up the creative workflow and help to get concept ideas out on paper. These ideas are then further refined and co developed with potential stake holders to create a truly usable, appealing and innovative design.

What’s next?

I am continuing to develop the device, incorporating sustainable design principles and potentially wireless sensors for broader environmental monitoring. Future iterations could leverage machine learning to interpret complex ecological data, creating a "dictionary" of plant signals. Ultimately, the aim to use this technology to foster a deeper connection with the natural world and inspire further exploration and innovation in organic computing.
