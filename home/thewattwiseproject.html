The WattWise Project

A conversational approach to embodied Energy Tracking

The WattWise Project is a tool designed to help you to understand and visualise how much embodied energy and carbon you consume on a daily basis across the categories of Food, The Home and Transportation. Providing actionable real time advice, to make more sustainable choices that don’t sacrifice on quality of life.

The Problem…

“What the hell is embodied energy?”

How might we find practical methods to reduce embodied energy & carbon footprints without reducing our quality of life?

  • In the face of escalating climate change, energy overconsumption and inefficient energy use pose significant barriers to global sustainability. The International Energy Agency highlights the urgent need for a major shift in consumer attitudes and behaviours towards energy efficiency to address challenges like carbon-intensive assets and the growing demand for clean energy.

    Despite advancements in renewable energy, public understanding of the impact of embodied energy and carbon consumption across a range of categories remains limited, hindering sustainable practices. It is precisely this pain point that decided to work towards a solution for with “The WattWise Project”.

The Solution - Introducing WattWise

An AI powered hardware & software platform that takes a conversational approach to embodied energy & carbon consumption tracking. Helping you to make more sustainable choices without sacrificing on quality of life.

  • WattWise is your energy-awareness companion, blending AI technology , a home energy consumption monitoring system and personalized advice to help you visualize the total amount of energy you use in a day across food, transport and the home.

    By combining the data available from these topics and your own input on your daily habits, WattWise offers real-time insights to build awareness of what your energy usage truly is, and offers advice to make small changes that have a big collective impact.

    WattWise aims to empower individuals to become aware of and embrace regenerative practices without sacrificing quality of life. Its purpose is making sustainability accessible and achievable for all.

WattWise can understand pictures, audio and normal text (in any language) so it’s able to identify and understand exactly what you are using and give you a detailed breakdown of your energy consumption.

Available through a telegram chat, WattWise AI is your personal consumption tracking companion

Paired with an easy to use plug and play home energy monitoring system, able to send detailed and accurate energy information straight to WattWise AI

How did we get here?

“How might we help people to keep track of and visualise their own embodied energy and carbon consumption?

  • We set out to develop a holistic tool that could track and visualize embodied energy consumption and carbon footprint across various aspects of daily life, including home, transportation, and food.

    We aimed to make sustainable living easier and more appealing by creating systems and tools that provided clear, actionable insights into energy use.

    Our goal was to bridge the gap between knowledge and action, encouraging people to adopt more sustainable habits through user-friendly and engaging solutions.

Exploration

First Person Perspective

Stakeholder surveys

Co Design Engagement

  • Our journey began with first-person perspective research. We started by tracking our own energy usage to gain first hand insights into the practical challenges and potential solutions. This was followed by surveys and community engagement in the Netherlands, UK, and Spain to gather public perceptions on energy usage and interest in tracking devices. Collaborative design sessions with fellow students and designers provided diverse perspectives on energy consumption and tracking methods.

First Person Perspective Research

Using first person perspective research methods to identify key pain points in the process of tracking embodied energy consumption.

  • This was the start of the project, a 24 hour challenge to see if it was possible to live beneath a self imposed limit of 2000 watts of total energy use for a day. This helped us to discover certain pain points that kickstarted our research.

    This led us to building a more dedicated prototype of an energy consumption tracking dashboard to make the consumption tracking easier.

    However, after 4 days of constant tracking, this method proved really ineffective, but we learnt a lot about the tracking process itself and where it worked and where it didn’t.

Co-Design Sessions

We used co-design sessions to bring some of our research participants and stakeholders along with us through the design process, making them part of the design process helped us to develop and iterate on solutions that suited their needs.

  • We need to shift sustainability messaging to positive encouragement rather than negative ones

  • How can we find ways to make climate change choices more playful and fun?

Stakeholder Surveys

At the same time, we held interviews and published surveys to get a wider view of the public’s perception around energy consumption habits and what they would need to be able to actively reduce their consumption.

Prototyping & Development

“could we use existing big data tools and artificial intelligence to do the heavy lifting for us, when paired with the right hardware?”

  • The pain points we had identified had shown us that while people were willing to make more sustainable habit changes, there was simply too much to be thinking about at once that ourselves and our research participants were suffering from Analysis Paralysis. Too much information and not knowing what the right decision is while having to keep track of everything was just too much. Resulting in no habit changes being done at all.

    Our Eureka moment came when we asked ourselves, “could we use existing big data tools and artificial intelligence to do the heavy lifting for us, when paired with the right hardware?

Hardware Experimentation

An Arduino based open source energy monitor

  • As a first step, we focused on the energy consumed in the home. We hacked an existing plug-in energy monitor to allow us to read the energy consumption data it was generating, then send that information to our software platform. Once this succeeded, we set about creating our version of an energy monitor that could be used to monitor the energy consumption of an entire home using a current transformer — just like a smart meter.

Prototyping our home energy consumption monitor

Software development

“The Home Energy Consumption Dashboard”

  • Our software platform started with us experimenting with an open source home automation platform called node-red. This node based platform is an excellent starting point for all kind of internet of things projects and gave us the platform we needed to experiment with visualising consumption data into something new and more appealing using a P5.js visualisation script and a dashboard.

The layout of our home energy consumption dashboard

The birth of “Dan The Energy Man”

Our first attempt at “talking” with our energy consumption

  • Our second (and arguably most important) lightbulb moment. After using our newly developed hardware and software combination that allowed us to visualise energy consumption levels in a different way, we realised it would be even easier if we could “talk” with our energy consumption levels.

    This is how Dan the Energy Man was born, using python to develop and connect a series of scripts together, we were able to create a database that would log energy consumption levels and send that data to a telegram bot that would put that information into a more human context.

The first communication stage of WattWise AI, asking in natural language about energy consumption status

Creating the development flows and mapping the functions of early WattWise

Advanced Prototyping

Prototyping a new kind of human-energy interaction, facilitated by AI language models and real time tracking…

  • After our initial success in creating a more intuitive method of communication with our energy consumption, we expanded the scope of our development. We realised that if we could use new AI tools like Large Language Models (LLMs) to do the energy consumption calculations, handle human language input (fuzzy input), use specifically curated data sets that we could create and provide, and output human language responses, paired with hardware that we could develop we could make a really unique and useful energy consumption tracking tool that could track not just home energy consumption, but the embodied energy in Food and Transport as well.

Hardware Iteration

A fully custom PCB design, with a stylish 3d printable housing

  • We continued to develop the energy monitor we’d designed, creating a new custom PCB design for our energy monitor that was adapted to work like a shield, where an Arduino could be plugged straight into the board and used right away. We also worked to develop a casing for this PCB so that it could be used as user facing prototype in the home.

    This casing was designed to be 3d printable so that it, and all the PCB files could be distributed around the world digitally and manufactured locally to reduce the footprint of the physical hardware as a consumer facing product, as well as keep the design open source to allow anyone to modify the files as needed to suit their specific circumstances.

we prototyped the custom PCB for the WattWise energy monitor and the manufactured the board in house

Testing out different iterations of the Hardware casings, using 3d printing and augmented reality

Software upgrade

“Dedicated AI powered insights”

  • On the software side, we started to develop a complete full stack solution, using Python to program our companion AI “WattWise”, which would be the main centrepiece of our prototype, a conversational assistant powered by the GPT-4o model from OpenAI, that would allow you to tell it exactly what you’ve been using throughout the day across Food, Transport and your Home energy consumption, to which it would respond by giving you a really precise breakdown of everything you’ve done, telling you exactly how much embodied energy and carbon you’ve consumed, as well as compare your consumption to your usage from previous days — something we found during testing that would be crucial for long term engagement with our users.

Usability Testing

Pilot Studies and Co Design Sessions

  • Throughout the entire development process, we were also testing the conversational AI concept with a 3 volunteers, each of them tracked a different category of energy consumption, and would send those results to us via text message, we would then feed their responses into an early version of our AI system, and send back the results.

    This process helped us to determine which kind of responses felt the most natural for our users as well as what information they prioritised the most. Bring some of our user base with us helped us to create a shared sense of ownership over the development of the solution where our volunteers felt like they were part of the solution development, which led to us gaining even more insight.

    Through a series of interviews and co design sessions they were able to leave their feedback for how the system could be further improved.

Co design session with the research participants of our week long WattWise AI pilot programme

Open Questions, Transparency and Responsibility

  • This is something that we asked ourselves a lot when developing this project, and we don’t have an answer yet, but maybe the answer lies in smaller and less power hungry models and setting up a hard energy consumption limit for our own system. Who knows, but we want to actively look to find an answer 😁🔎

What’s next?

The project is currently in a working prototype stage, and is currently being tested in a more long term capacity by a few volunteers, to see how we can further improve engagement and see if the prototype continues to help our users reduce their energy consumption and carbon footprint without reducing their quality of life.

We want to continue developing The WattWise Project and share it with the community. We want to gather broader feedback, explore how we can use gamification to maintain engagement, see if the idea can apply to businesses, and test scalability with open source and less resource intensive models.
